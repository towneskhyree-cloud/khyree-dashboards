Preface

Of the Chair, the Hour,
and the Manner of Things

The cut takes forty-five minutes. The experience — what the client remembers two weeks later when a stranger asks where they get their hair done — that is built from seven minutes total, spread across the seven movements recorded in this codex. Every barber gives the cut. Almost none give the ceremony. The gap between the two is the entire business.
What follows is not a list of best practices. It is a protocol — a sequence of specific gestures, words, and small expenses, performed in a specific order, that turns the act of cutting hair into something a person tells other people about. The unit of work is not the cut. It is the memory the client leaves with.
Total added cost per client: under two dollars. Total added time: under five minutes. Net effect over twelve months: tips lift by 35–60%, in-chair rebook rate triples, word-of-mouth referral becomes the primary acquisition channel. The ceremony is the cheapest revenue lever in the room.
ARTICLE I

The Welcome

T − 5 to T + 0. The first 90 seconds. Where the cut is won before it begins.

Before any blade is drawn, the client crosses a threshold. What occurs in the ninety seconds that follow determines the tip, the rebook, and whether they will speak your name to a stranger. Treat this minute as the first cut, even though no hair has been touched.

ARTICLE II

The Consultation

T + 1 to T + 5. Five questions, asked before the cape falls. The questions that earn the upcharge.

The cape goes on last, not first. Before it is draped, you ask. Most barbers ask one question — "what are we doing today" — and proceed. The Khyree City consultation is five questions, in order, with an outcome assigned to each. The cape comes off the hook only after the fifth answer is received.

ARTICLE III

The Finish

T + 40 to T + 43. Three minutes that justify the upgrade. The cheapest luxury in the building.

The cut concludes. Most barbers brush, uncape, swipe payment. The Khyree City ceremony adds three minutes of finishing ritual — hot towel, scalp pressure, oil — that costs the house under fifty cents and lifts the perceived value of the service by an order of magnitude. This is where the tip is built.

ARTICLE IV

The Photograph

T + 43 to T + 45. Three frames. One backdrop. Sent in sixty seconds. Posted by Sunday.

Every cut produces three photographs. Front, left profile, right profile. Same backdrop, same light, same angle each time, so that the body of work across hundreds of clients reads as one continuous portfolio rather than a scrapbook. The frames are sent to the client within sixty seconds — before they leave the chair. They post the photograph. Their network sees you. You did not pay for that reach.

ARTICLE V

The Re-Booking

T + 44. Before the cape lifts. The highest-intent moment of the entire client lifetime.

The client has just received the towel, the scalp press, the oil, the photographs. They are still in the chair. This is the moment. Not when they reach for their wallet. Not when they stand at the door. Before the cape lifts. The next slot — four weeks, same day, same time — is offered in a single sentence, with your phone already open to the calendar.
The Closing Sentence

ARTICLE VI

The Departure

T + 45. They are walked to the door. They are handed something. They are remembered.

The cut has ended. They have paid. They have rebooked. The temptation is to release them at the chair. Do not. The departure is the final scene — what they replay on the drive home. Walk them to the door. Hand them something. Speak the rebook out loud, one more time, into the air between you. This is how a service becomes a relationship.

ARTICLE VII

The Memory

T + 45 to ∞. The notebook. What you record. Why memory is the most expensive luxury you can give.

The cut has ended. The client has departed. The ceremony is not over. The final movement happens after they leave. You return to the chair, open the booking app, and record three lines in their profile that have nothing to do with their hair. This is the most undervalued act in the trade.
Their kids' names. The trip they mentioned. The promotion they were nervous about. The book they were reading. None of it is for sale. All of it is for memory. Six weeks from now, when they return, you mention one of those three details by name. The look on their face is the entire return on the thirty seconds you spent writing it down.
The Notebook Protocol
Three Lines, Always
Iline

One detail about their life that has nothing to do with hair. Kids. Work. The trip. The fight with their brother. The thing they said off-handedly that most barbers forget by the next client.

One detail to follow up on next time. "Ask about the job interview." "Ask if the trip went well." "Ask how Naomi's birthday went." Written today. Read aloud six weeks from now. The client believes you have a memory like an elephant. You have a notebook like a craftsman.

10 secper profile

Three lines. Fifty seconds. The memory is the most expensive luxury you can give a client, and it costs nothing. The Khyree City booking app reserves a notes field on every client record for exactly this purpose. Fill it. Every time. Without exception.

FOLIO IV · APPENDIX B

The House Rules

Nine standards. Non-negotiable. The contract between the chair and the work performed in it.

I

The Phone Is Face-Down

Once the cape falls, the phone stays down. No calls. No checks. No glances. The hour the client paid for is the hour the client receives — undivided.

II

The Client Is Named

Every client is greeted by name within thirty seconds of entry. If you do not remember the name, the app does. There is no excuse.

III

The Consultation Precedes The Cape

Five questions, in order. The cape does not fall until the fifth answer is received. The questions are the work.

IV

The Finish Is Not Optional

Hot towel, scalp press, finishing oil. Every cut. Every tier. The Authentic gets the same finish as the Reserve. The tier difference is duration, not care.

V

The Photograph Is Within Sixty Seconds

Three frames. Same backdrop. Sent before the cape lifts. No exception. The chair's marketing is built one photograph at a time.

VI

The Rebook Is Offered In-Chair

Phone open to the calendar. Next slot offered before the cape lifts. Not at the door. Not in a follow-up text. In the chair, while the towel is still warm.

VII

The Memory Is Recorded

Three lines, every client, every cut. Personal, technical, future. The notebook is not optional — it is the difference between a barber and a craftsman.

VIII

The House Is Halal

No false testimonials. No invented numbers. No urgency manipulation. The work earns the next booking, or it does not. What is real, stays. What is fabricated, never enters.

IX

The Client Is Walked To The Door

You do not release the client at the chair. You walk with them. You hold the door. You speak the rebook one final time. The departure is the final scene they replay.
