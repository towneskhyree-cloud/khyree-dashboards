— THE HOUSE · CUT CREATERS

A house that existed before the chair did.

Built in 1996, when the Decatur scene was a handful of corner shops and the building was an experiment. Three decades later — 140 suites, one address, the longest-running independent operator house in the metro.

The chair runs out of a single suite on the second floor. Most clients walk through the lobby past forty other working barbers, stylists, and braiders before the elevator. It is, on purpose, not a salon. It is a building of small businesses.

FOLIO II · THE STORY · THE TRADE

Born in the chair.
Raised in the trade.

The Trade
The chair was in the family before I was. I grew up around it. The smell of clipper oil, the snap of the cape, my father's voice calling the next name. I picked up the clippers at nine. I've cut professionally for two decades. This is the only thing I have ever done well.

Quality is remembered long after the price is forgotten.

— The thesis of the house —
SECTION
01

Three things the work runs on.

The chair is a small business. Small businesses survive on three disciplines done daily. Skip one, and the chair starts to lean.

PRINCIPLE01

The phone is face-down.

Once the cape falls, the phone stays down. No calls. No checks. No glances. The hour the client paid for is the hour the client receives, undivided.

PRINCIPLE02

The client is named.

Every client greeted by name within thirty seconds of entry. If memory fails, the notebook doesn't. There is no excuse for "what's up, man."

PRINCIPLE03

The finish is not optional.

Hot towel. Scalp press. Finishing oil. Every cut. Every tier. The Lineup gets the same care as the Reserve — the tier difference is duration, not attention.

SECTION
02

The chair itself.

One station, three feet of clear floor, fluorescent natural-tone overhead, matte black photo panel at the back. Designed for the work, not the photograph. The photograph is a byproduct.

The chair is a station, not a stage.

Built around the work the hands do, not the photograph the phone takes. The light is calibrated to read texture. The mirror is angled so the client sees the same face the operator does. The notebook lives at the right hand. The phone is on silent, face-down, the entire shift.

The matte black panel at the back is not décor. It is the photo studio for every cut — same backdrop, same light, same angle. Frame 001 looks like frame 001,000 because the geometry of the room does not change.

The Reserve appointments happen after-hours. The lobby is quiet. The building is mostly empty. The client gets a different version of the chair than the daytime clients do — slower, longer, with a finishing oil that does not appear on the standard menu.

By hand.
For a long time.

The work has been in the family since the building was opened in 1996. My father, Omar, has cut hair for thirty years. I'm not building from scratch. I'm taking a seat that was already warm.

Most barbers learn the trade as a job. I learned it as a household sound. The buzz of the clippers in the kitchen. The smell of the towel warmer. The way my father called the next client to the chair without raising his voice. That's the version of the work I'm trying to keep alive — slow, deliberate, owned.

The chair has a history. It does not have to mention it constantly. The work proves the lineage.
