SECTION01

The thesis: the wordmark is the logo.

Hormozi's Acquisition.com. Stripe. Vercel. Off-White. Supreme. Linear. None of them put their product inside their logo. None of them have a "moneybag" or a "computer" or a "shopping cart." The most expensive brands in any category default to a wordmark with one strong typographic decision. The discipline IS the design.

01 / THE OLD WAYLogos as decoration

Most small-business logos try to illustrate the service. A scissors. A comb. A chair silhouette. A barbershop pole. This is the equivalent of putting a picture of a car on a Ferrari badge. The brand has to do the work the logo can't.

Generic, decorative logos signal one thing to a sophisticated buyer: this person paid $40 on Fiverr. That signal is the kiss of death for premium pricing. A $200 cut needs a logo that doesn't look like it cost $40.

02 / THE NEW WAYLogos as discipline

The serious brands treat the logo as a typographic decision. They pick one font. They commit one weight. They make one custom letter cut — or none. They use one color. The restraint is what reads as money.

A great wordmark says one thing: "we know who we are and we don't need a picture to prove it." That single signal is worth more than every illustrated barber logo on the internet combined.

SECTION02

Nine principles the logo has to pass.

These are not opinions. They are filters. Every candidate logo gets run through all nine. If it fails any one, it goes back to the desk. No exceptions, including the ones you fell in love with.

PRINCIPLE 01

It works in black and white.

If the logo only works in color, it's not a logo. Pure black on white and pure white on black are the only two tests that matter first. Color comes second.

PRINCIPLE 02

It works at 16 pixels.

The favicon test. If you can't recognize it as a 16px square in a browser tab, it has too much detail. Reduce until it survives.

PRINCIPLE 03

It works on a suite door.

Cut Creaters, Suite 47. At 30 inches across, in a dim hallway, etched in brass or printed on vinyl. Does it still feel right? If yes, keep going.

PRINCIPLE 04

It has zero effects.

No gradients. No drop shadows. No bevels. No "shine." If the logo needs effects to look good, the underlying mark is weak. Strip it.

PRINCIPLE 05

One bold decision.

Stripe's custom letterforms. Vercel's triangle. Off-White's quotation marks. One distinctive move that makes it ownable. Not three. Not five. One.

PRINCIPLE 06

It doesn't depict the service.

No scissors. No combs. No chairs. No fades. No razor blades. The wordmark doesn't have to prove it's a barber brand. The work proves it.

PRINCIPLE 07

It can be re-drawn by hand.

If someone with a sharpie can sketch it on a napkin in 5 seconds, it's iconic. If it requires a pen plotter and 14 layers, it isn't. Memorability is shape, not detail.

PRINCIPLE 08

It survives 10 years.

No trend signifiers. No "current year" effects. No gradients du jour. Imagine the logo in 2034. Does it still look intentional? Or does it look like 2024?

PRINCIPLE 09

The name is louder than the mark.

If someone sees the logo and can't say the name out loud, the logo failed. Khyree City is the brand. The mark is an accessory. The name does 90% of the work.

SECTION03

What to reject on sight.

Every AI logo generator and every $40 Fiverr "designer" will hand back the same six clichés. Knowing what to reject is half the brief.

REJECT IMMEDIATELY

The barber cliché stack

  • Scissors crossed with a comb
  • A barbershop pole, in any orientation
  • A silhouette of a man with a fade
  • A straight razor as the I in "city"
  • A gradient mesh going from blue to purple
  • Cursive script that "looks classy" (it doesn't)
  • The word "BARBER" in any font
  • A crown above a K
  • A mustache anywhere on the page
  • Any of the 14 free icons on flaticon.com tagged "barber"
ACCEPT FOR REVIEW

The disciplined wordmark

  • Heavy geometric sans-serif, all-caps, tight tracking
  • One custom letter cut — usually the K
  • One punctuation move: a period, an em-dash, brackets
  • A monogram (KC) inside a square or circle, in mono weight
  • An abstract geometric mark referencing one shape
  • Type set in stencil or condensed industrial
  • Pure black and pure white only
  • Negative space carrying the visual interest
  • A version that works without ever showing a barbering object
  • Anything that could survive next to Stripe in a footer

SECTION06

How to judge what you get back.

Whatever the tool generates — Midjourney, Ideogram, a freelancer, even me in a follow-up — every candidate runs through these nine questions in order. If a candidate fails any one, it goes back. No exceptions for the ones you fell in love with.

Q 01

Survives black + white?

Strip the color. Does the logo still read? If it needs color to work, reject it.

Q 02

Survives 16 pixels?

Shrink it to favicon size. Can you still recognize it? If detail vanishes, reject it.

Q 03

Survives 30 inches?

Scale it to the size of a suite door. Does it still feel intentional, or does it look stretched and empty?

Q 04

Zero effects?

Any drop shadow, gradient, bevel, or "shine"? Reject. The mark itself must do the work.

Q 05

One bold decision?

Can you name the single distinctive move in one sentence? If not, the logo is forgettable.

Q 06

Zero barber clichés?

Scissors, combs, poles, fades, crowns, capes — any of them anywhere? Reject without discussion.

Q 07

Re-drawable by hand?

Could a 12-year-old sketch a recognizable version with a sharpie in 5 seconds? If no, too complex.

Q 08

Ages 10 years?

Picture the logo in 2034. Does it still look intentional, or does it look like 2024 trend signaling?

Q 09

Name > mark?

When someone sees the logo, do they read "KHYREE CITY" first, or do they look at decoration first?

Pick one direction. Reject five. Refine until the logo disappears into the brand.

"A great logo is one you stop seeing — because the brand has eaten it. Until then, the discipline IS the design."
